Quora - General College Freshman Tips
Source: quora.com

Take care of your physical needs: eat decently, sleep regularly, exercise, get mental rest. Use academic resources — professors have office hours and like to help students. Use them BEFORE you are failing and desperate. Tutors are often available for free from learning centers.

Stop procrastinating. Now. You can't take the same approach as in high school. Some projects can't be done in a night. Your brain won't do it. Start every course as if everything depends on it. Recovering from a lazy start is very difficult.

Talk to people. It's the best way to find out which classes and professors are good. Talk to your academic advisor. Explore different areas as a freshman — you can still graduate on time even if you switch majors. Much harder as a junior to suddenly realize you like something different.

Focus on three things: first, attend and pass all your classes. Second, practice self-care — keeping yourself alive, healthy, fed, clean, and rested. Third, make some friends or at least develop connections with people you genuinely have things in common with. About 40% of students don't achieve all three of these, and many end up not finishing college.

It's not high school — you can't just float and then cram for an exam. The goal is not to accumulate points for a grade. Focus on learning the material from day one. Topics build on themselves and not understanding something early limits what you can understand later. Probably the most important thing to learn your first year is time management.

Make good use of office hours. It can really help if you 'click' with a professor and they become an unofficial mentor. Those conversations can carry over to other courses. Decades later, some professors are still in contact with students they first met as freshmen.

Value your mental health. About half of college freshmen don't graduate. We're in a time where everyone is trying to go to college, making competitive majors more competitive. Don't value a degree so much that you destroy your mental health over it. There is no shame in going at your own pace. Pace yourself. Eat well. Sleep. If you're going to bother with college, make sure you genuinely enjoy the field.

Prioritize a consistent sleep schedule of 7-9 hours. Cognitive performance and memory retention degrade quickly with chronic sleep loss.

Build a class-week rhythm: review lecture notes within 24 hours, do active recall twice before exams, and summarize each week in 10-15 minutes.

Use the syllabus as a contract: record every assignment, exam, and grade weight in one calendar at semester start and plan backward from deadlines.

Prefer spaced repetition and active practice like flashcards, problem sets, and practice exams over last-minute rereading.

One master calendar plus one weekly to-do list reduces task loss. Block study times and protect them like appointments.

Balance course load: mix a challenging major requirement with one lower-stress elective. Avoid stacking all hard courses the same term.

Meet instructors during office hours before the first big assignment. A 10-minute introduction improves grades and mentorship options.

If struggling academically, act the week you notice it. Small interventions like a study group or TA session prevent large grade drops.

Create a simple semester budget: income, fixed expenses, discretionary spending, and emergency buffer.

Use student discounts for software, transit, and memberships. Compare textbook costs — buy used, rent, or use library copies before buying new.

Join 1-2 clubs or activities aligned with interests instead of overcommitting. Regular attendance builds meaningful connections.

Learn campus mental health options like counseling and crisis lines. Normalizing help-seeking reduces escalation.

Maintain a baseline routine that includes exercise, social contact, and downtime. Treat these as part of academic strategy, not distractions.

Set clear expectations with roommates early: guests, cleaning, quiet hours, shared expenses. Put agreements in writing.

Start career exploration early: informational interviews, campus career fair attendance, and LinkedIn profile creation in freshman year.

Get a faculty contact by contributing to class and attending office hours. Mentors open research and recommendation opportunities.

Participate in events on campus. Events are the perfect opportunity to do something fun, make new friends, and take a break from studying.

Go to class. In college there is freedom because professors don't always take attendance, but don't make the mistake of missing classes, especially your first year. Participating in class will help you achieve great grades.

Get an internship even if it's not required for your major. It can be life-changing and helps you discover what you actually like and don't like.

Don't be afraid to ask for help, whether with a class you don't understand or stress management. There are people to help — professors, RAs, counseling offices.

Live in the moment. Allow yourself to have fun and do hobbies you love. This keeps stress levels down and gives you something to look forward to outside of class.
